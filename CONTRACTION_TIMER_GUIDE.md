## What is the Contraction Timer?

The Contraction Timer helps you track labor contractions to know when it's time to go to the hospital. It automatically:
- ✅ Times each contraction duration
- ✅ Calculates time between contractions (frequency)
- ✅ Tracks statistics (average duration, frequency, count)
- ✅ Alerts you when contractions match the **5-1-1 rule**
- ✅ Keeps a complete history you can share with your doctor

---

## How to Use

### Starting Your First Contraction

1. **Navigate to Contraction Timer**
   - From Dashboard, tap "Contractions" in Safety & Planning section
   - Or use the More menu (bottom navigation)

2. **When a contraction starts:**
   - Tap the **"Start Contraction"** button
   - The timer will begin counting
   - The circle will pulse to show it's active

3. **When the contraction ends:**
   - Tap the **"End Contraction"** button
   - The contraction is automatically saved
   - Statistics update immediately

4. **Continue timing:**
   - After a contraction ends, press "Start Contraction" again for the next one
   - The app automatically calculates time between contractions

### Resetting the Timer

- If you accidentally started the timer, tap **"Reset Timer"**
- This only clears the current timing, not your history

## Understanding the 5-1-1 Rule

### What is it?

The **5-1-1 rule** is a guideline for when to go to the hospital:
- **5** — Contractions are **5 minutes apart**
- **1** — Each contraction lasts **1 minute** (45-90 seconds)
- **1** — This pattern continues for **1 hour**

### Why it matters

This pattern usually means you're in **active labor** and should:
- ✅ Call your doctor or midwife
- ✅ Head to the hospital
- ✅ Prepare for delivery

### Important Notes

⚠️ **This is a guideline, not a rule!** Your doctor may give you different instructions based on:
- Your pregnancy history
- Distance from hospital
- Previous births (faster for 2nd+ babies)
- Any complications

**Always follow your doctor's specific instructions!**

## When to Call Your Doctor

### Immediate Call Needed:

1. **Water Breaks**
   - Clear, pink, or greenish fluid
   - Even if no contractions

2. **Regular Contractions**
   - 5 minutes apart for 1 hour
   - Or following your doctor's instructions

3. **Severe Pain**
   - Pain that doesn't ease between contractions
   - Sharp, constant pain

4. **Bleeding**
   - More than light spotting
   - Bright red blood

5. **Baby's Movement**
   - Movements decrease significantly
   - No movement for several hours

6. **Other Warning Signs**
   - Severe headache
   - Vision changes (blurry, spots)
   - Sudden swelling (face, hands, feet)
   - Fever over 100.4°F (38°C)

### Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Before labor, discuss:
- When should I start timing contractions?
- When should I call you?
- When should I go to the hospital?
- Should I eat or drink during early labor?
- Can I take a shower during early labor?

## Labor Stages Guide

### Stage 1: Early Labor

**Contractions:**
- 5-30 minutes apart
- 30-45 seconds long
- Mild to moderate intensity

**What to Do:**
- ✅ Stay home and rest
- ✅ Eat light, easy-to-digest foods
- ✅ Stay hydrated
- ✅ Take a warm shower
- ✅ Practice breathing exercises
- ✅ Time contractions occasionally

**How Long:**
- First baby: 8-12 hours (average)
- Second+ baby: 4-8 hours (average)

---

### Stage 2: Active Labor

**Contractions:**
- 3-5 minutes apart
- 45-60 seconds long
- Strong intensity

**What to Do:**
- ✅ Go to hospital
- ✅ Practice breathing techniques
- ✅ Change positions frequently
- ✅ Use pain relief options
- ✅ Stay hydrated (ice chips)

**How Long:**
- First baby: 4-8 hours
- Second+ baby: 2-4 hours

---

### Stage 3: Transition

**Contractions:**
- 2-3 minutes apart
- 60-90 seconds long
- Very intense

**What to Do:**
- ✅ Focus on breathing
- ✅ Rest between contractions
- ✅ Accept support
- ✅ Remind yourself: almost there!

**How Long:**
- 30 minutes to 2 hours

---

### Stage 4: Pushing

**Contractions:**
- 2-5 minutes apart
- 60-90 seconds long
- Strong urge to push

**What to Do:**
- ✅ Push with contractions
- ✅ Rest between pushes
- ✅ Follow medical team's guidance

**How Long:**
- First baby: 30 min - 3 hours
- Second+ baby: Few minutes - 1 hour

## Export Your Data

### Why Export?

Share your contraction pattern with your doctor for:
- Better diagnosis
- Treatment decisions
- Complete medical record

### How to Export

1. Go to Contraction Timer
2. Scroll to **Contraction History** section
3. Tap **"Export"** button
4. CSV file downloads automatically
5. Share via email, WhatsApp, or print

### What's in the Export?

CSV file includes:
- Date of each contraction
- Time of each contraction
- Duration (in seconds)
- Frequency (minutes between contractions)

## Troubleshooting

### Timer Won't Start

**Solution:**
- Refresh the page
- Check if browser is up to date
- Try different browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari)

### Notifications Not Working

**Solution:**
1. Check if notifications are enabled:
   - Settings → Notifications → Allow
2. Grant permission when prompted
3. Check phone's Do Not Disturb mode

### Can't See History

**Solution:**
- History stored in browser's localStorage
- Clearing browser data will delete history
- Export regularly to keep a backup

### Wrong Time Between Contractions

**Solution:**
- Make sure to press "End Contraction" for each one
- First contraction won't have frequency (no previous to compare)
- Delete incorrect entries and re-time if needed

### 5-1-1 Alert Not Showing

**Solution:**
- Alert requires at least 6 contractions in 1 hour
- Contractions must be 3-7 minutes apart
- Duration must be 45-90 seconds
- If pattern doesn't match, alert won't trigger

### Lost My Data

**Solution:**
- Data is stored in browser
- Clearing browser data or switching devices will lose history
- **Prevention:** Export regularly to CSV

## Tips for Best Results

### Before Labor

1. **Practice Now**
   - Familiarize yourself with the timer
   - Try timing Braxton Hicks contractions

2. **Set Up Notifications**
   - Enable push notifications
   - Test that they work

3. **Discuss with Doctor**
   - Show them the app
   - Ask when to start timing
   - Get their specific guidelines

### During Labor

1. **Don't Obsess Over Timing**
   - Time periodically, not constantly
   - Focus on breathing and relaxing

2. **Get Help**
   - Have partner or support person help time
   - You focus on managing contractions

3. **Trust Your Instincts**
   - If something feels wrong, call doctor
   - Don't wait for "perfect" 5-1-1 pattern

4. **Stay Hydrated & Rested**
   - Drink water during early labor
   - Rest between contractions
   - Save energy for active labor

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: When should I start using the timer?

**A:** Start timing when:
- Contractions feel regular
- They're becoming stronger
- You're wondering "is this it?"

Don't time constantly — every 30-60 minutes is enough in early labor.

### Q: What if my contractions don't follow a pattern?

**A:** This is normal in early labor. As labor progresses, they'll become more regular. If concerned, call your doctor.

### Q: Should I time Braxton Hicks?

**A:** You can practice with Braxton Hicks, but real labor contractions:
- Get stronger over time
- Get closer together
- Don't stop when you change position
- May include back pain

### Q: What if I have to go to hospital before 5-1-1?

**A:** Your doctor may tell you to come in sooner if:
- You live far from hospital
- This is your second+ baby
- You have complications
- Your water breaks

Always follow your doctor's specific plan!

### Q: Can I use this for false labor?

**A:** Yes! Timing contractions helps determine if it's false labor (Braxton Hicks) or real labor.

**False Labor:**
- Irregular timing
- Don't get closer together
- Stop with rest or position change
- Usually painless or mild

**Real Labor:**
- Regular pattern
- Get closer together
- Get stronger
- Don't stop with rest
